poverty and wealth

Word Note
penury Latin penuria: need, neediness
dire, abject poverty, complete lack of financial resources
one of two strongest English words there are to denote absence of money
penurious adjective of penury
stingy, close-fisted, niggardly (sparing in the use of money as to give the appearance of penury)
penurousness stinginess, excessive frugality
parsimonious stingy, but milder than penurious
parsimony the noun form of parsimonious
indigence poverty, milder than penury
not absolutely penniless, simply living in a reduced circumstances
indigent the adjective form of destitution
destitution poverty, as strong as penury
do not even have the means for mere subsistence
destitute the adjective form of destitution
affluence more than comfortable; there is the implication that their wealth is increasing
Latin fluo: to flow
prefix ad-: changing to af- before a root beginning with f
money keeps flowing to us
affluent
confluence a flowing together
opulence not only implies much greater wealth than affluence, but in addition suggests lavish expeditures and ostentatiously luxurious surroundings
Latin opulentus: wealthy
opulent the adjective form of opulence, may describe people, surroundings, styles of life, or the like

short time

Word Note
ephemera anything so short-lived, so unenduring that it scarcely seems to outlast the day
evanescent synonym of ephemeral
something intagible, like a feeling, may be called evanescent
prefix e-: out
vanesco: to vanish
suffix -ent: adjective
suffix -escent: becoming or beginning to
begnin to vanish
evanescence suffix -ence: begin to
evanesce
adolescent beginning to become an adult
convalesce begin to get well after illness
putrescent beginning to rot; beginning to become putrid
obsolescent becoming obsolete
fleeting synonym of evanescent

good

Word Note
euphemism a word or expression that has been substituted for another that is likely to offend
Greek prefix eu-: good
pheme: voice
suffix -ism: noun
something said in a good voice
euphemistic
euphony good ound, pleasant lilt or rhythm
phone: sound
euphonic
euphonious
eulogy good speech
a formal speech of praise, usually delivered as a funeral oration
logos: word or speech
monologue only one man is speaking
epilogue words upon the other words, or “after-words”
prologue words before the main part, “before-words”
eulogistic
eulogize
eulogist
euphoria good feeling, a sense of mental buoyancy and physical well-being
euphoric
euthanasia good death
method of painless death inflicted on people suffering from incurable diseases
Greek thanatos: death

expression

Word Note
badinage a half-teasing, non-malicious, frivolous banter, intended to amuse rather than wound
persiflage synonym of badinage
a little more derisive, a trifle mroe indicative of contempt or mockery, but still totally unmalicious
cliché a pattern of words which was once new and fresh, but which now is so old, worn, and threadbare that only banal, unimaginative speakers and writers ever use it
bromide any trite, dull, and probably fallacious remark that shows little evidence of original thinking, and that therefore convinces a listener of the total absence of perspicacity on the part of the speaker
a drug, formerly used as a sedative
bromidic the adjective form of bromide
platitude a dull, trite, hackneyed, unimaginative pattern of words, but, to add insult to injury, the speaker uses it with an air of novelty — as if he just made it up and isn’t he the brilliant fellow
Greek platys: broad or flat
suffix -tude: noun
platitudicous
plateau flat land
platter flat dishes
platypus flat foot
anodyne in the medical sense, is a drug that allays pain without curing an illness
figuratively, an anodyne is a statement made to allay someone’s fears or anxienties, not believed by the speaker, but intended to be believed by the listener
prefix an-: negative
Greek odyne: pain

animals

Word Note
bovine Latin bovis: ox or cow
suffix -ine: like, similar to, characteristic of
far from complimentary, implies a certain mild contempt on the part of the speaker
phlegmatic self-controlled
milder than bovine
leonine like a lion in appearance or temperament
Latin leo: lion
canine as an adejctive, like a dog
as a noun, the species to which dogs belong
Latin canis: dog
feline catlike
Latin felis: cat
porcine piglike
Latin porcus: pig
vulpine foxlike in appearance or temperament. When applied to people , this adjective usually indicates the shrewdness of a fox
Latin vulpus: fox
ursine bearlike
Latin ursus: bear
lupine wolflike
Latin lupus: wolf
equine horselike
Latin equus: horse
piscine fishlike
Latin piscis: fish

cardialgia heartburn
nostalgic

sounds

Word Note
cacophony Greek kakos: bad, harsh, or uly
cacophonous
phonograph writer of sound
saxophone a musical instrument invented by Adolphe Sax
xylophone a musical instrument
Greek xylon: wood
sounds through wood
phonetics the science of the sounds of language
phonetic
phonetician
phonics the science of sound
the method of teaching reading by drilling the sounds of letters and syllables

Word Note
carnivorous carnis: flesh
voro: to devour
the main diet of a carnivorous animal is meat
carnivore carnivorous animal
herbivorous subsisting on grains, grasses, and other vegetation
Latin herba: herb
herbivore
omnivorous eating everything

omnivorous reader reads everything in great quantities
omnipotent all-powerful
an adjective usually applied to God; also to any ruler whose governing powers are unlimited
Latin potens, potentis: powerful
omnipotence

impotent powerless
potent powerful
potential possessing power or ability not yet exercised
omniscient all-knowing, infinitely wise
sciens: knowing
omniscience

ubiquitous Latin ubique: everywhere

ubiquity

bus

voracity

carnival a kind of outdoor entertainment with games, rides, side sows, lots of food
